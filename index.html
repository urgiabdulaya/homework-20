Design Process

I used a lean UX design thinking approach to make sure my choices were backed up by user research and feedbacks.

Background

For my UX/UI bootcamp at the University of Minnesota, my classmates and I had to pick a nonprofit organization to create a Responsive Web Design. As we were looking for a nonprofit organization to redesign, my teammate suggested taking a look at CAIR-MN’s current website. As my teammates and I spent some time scrolling through CAIR-MN’s website, we noticed some problems surrounds a lack of accessibility, their website being unresponsive to other devices, an unresponsive user flow, and their website feeling disorganized. As we scrolled through the website, we were able to pinpoint exactly what needed to be fixed, but still had a hard time understand who CAIR-MN is and what their organization is all about. After sometime scrolling through their website, we were finally able to understand who CAIR-MN is and their mission. CAIR-MN’s mission is to enhance the understand of Islam, protect civil rights, promote justice, and empower American Muslims. As a Muslim American myself, this project was personal. I was able to resonate with each person I interviewed and was really motivated to create a masterpiece.

Phase one: Discovery

User Interviews & Surveys

I decided to conduct user interviews, along with online surveys, as this topic is sensitive and people may not feel comfortable sharing their true experiences during a 1:1 interview. For my user interviews, I interviewed five different users who identifies as Muslim Americans, as well as reside in Minnesota. The user interviews consisted of questions surrounding the participants experiences as Muslim Americans and ways they may have coped upon their civil rights being violated. I recorded all the answers during the interview (with their permission), which later helped me analyze the users responses. Along with user interviews, users also participated in a online survey. I surveyed a total of 23 participants are based in Minnesota, as well as identify as Muslim Americans. With the survey, I wanted to get a better understanding of who CAIR-MN users are and their experiences as a Muslim American. Since this is a sensitive and personal topic, I was able to get more participants through the survey than the user interviews. The survey consisted of questions surrounding who the users are, if they are familiar with CAIR-MN and if so, what CAIR-MN resources have they used, if users have experienced discrimination due to their religion, as well as where the discrimination took place.

Phase three: Ideate 

Card Sorting

I performed a closed card sorting exercise with a few of my potential users to get a clear understanding of how they would perceive the websites flow. Objective: Understand how users conceptualize and mentally associate certain categories Task: Users were shown a list of navigation headings and secondary navigation options that would be listed on the website. They were asked to categories the navigation headings with the secondary navigation option that they might associate together.

Usability testing

Usability testing were done via zoom. I wrote a script and protocol for the testing process and asked users to perform certain task, identify clickable parts of the prototype and give their observations and feedback. During the usability testing, I avoided giving instructions to the user or influencing the interviewee to answer in a particular way. My main task as the interviewer was to sit back and observe the interviewee as they completed their assigned task. Test objectives: To test navigation flow and identify pinpoints surrounding accessibility challenges. Overarching question: How easy or difficult is it for users to navigate and complete the task? Is CAIR-MN’s layout easy to figure out?

A/B testing & KPI

A/B testing were conducted via Zoom with ten users. Upon completing our first usability testing, we found that users clicked on the “know your rights” button rather that searching for it through the navigation bar. This led us to wonder if it’s necessary to keep both the “know your rights” button and navigation bar in the home page. In order to determine which one users benefit more from, we decided to do an A/B test, as well as measure users KPI upon completing the task given to users. The UX design team created two versions of the CAIR-MN home page and know your rights page. The A version consisted of the home page “Know your rights” button, as well as being in the navigation bar. The B version consisted of users having to navigate to the “know your rights” page by using the navigation bar only, which mean they no longer had access to the “know your rights” button.

Impact

The findings from this research helped the design team iterate and implement a new UI design for CAIR-MN that will align closely with users’ needs and diminish pain points users face when using their current website.

Next Steps

To ensure that users’ needs are being met, I would like to do some research on if it would be beneficial for CAIR-MN to implement a language selector. Throughout my research, I noticed many of the individuals CAIR-MN cater to are not native English speakers. This made me wonder, how many individuals click on the CAIR-MN website but get stuck due to English being their second language?
